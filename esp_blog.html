A year-long Computer Science module in which students, in groups of 8, were tasked with identifying a problem, interacting with stakeholders, and coming up with a solution across 4 deliverables. The "problem" could be anything, as long as it could be nestled into one of a few (very broad) genres, mind. The module was intended to mimic the development lifecycle of a product in industry, albeit on a significantly smaller scale, but groups were nonetheless encouraged to take the same steps that professional teams would when developing a system - roles, responsibilities, scrum meetings and sprints, the whole package.

Our group of 8 identified several problems but settled on student-society engagement, which here refers to the plethora of ways students interact with their societies, and vice versa, at university. We're talking freshers' events at the very start of the year, sports fairs, special society events, taster sessions, etc.

A very significant amount of time was spent surveying, planning, interviewing, and surveying again to build the base of the project and to guide our trajectory as a group. We had many more, all organized according to priority in the final system as it was emphasized by our tutors that the development portion of the project would be a bit of a time crunch, made worse by the fact of the year surrounding by other exams. It was probably that we wouldn't get around to it all, but ultimately that wasn't entirely the point of the module, so that's absolutely fine - it's about developing a plan, and sticking to that plan whilst you battle the little hurdles that pop up along the way.

We had outlined the purpose and various functions of the system, and I took responsibility for investigating, testing, and teaching about the technologies we would use for development. While the rest of the team had primarily worked with Python, I had spent time outside of uni exploring other technologies courtesy of curiosity inspired by Stack Overflow's annual surveys. I was already roughly aware of what technologies I thought we'd need during development, so I conducted some preliminary tests and created tutorials to give to the rest of the group.

I focused on the notoriously troublesome topics, giving walkthroughs and talks after our weekly scrum meetings on topics like GIT (conventions of branching, merging, frequent commits, and the most common commands), remote version control, as well as our plan for testing and deployment. I provided the group with an overview of our system design, React (and why we should use it), Flask, and Docker.

I spent a great deal of time setting up the initial images and repositories before walking the group through how we would use Docker containers to emulate a more massive system distributed across multiple servers, to insulate the system from our personal machines for standardized testing, as well as to establish remote deployments for testing. We could unify our working environments, distribute tests, and finally deploy the system to Digital Ocean droplets funded by their free credits... The meetings provided an in-person environment for me to help the other developers set everything up on their machines and answer any questions.

Development was largely uneventful, thanks to the planning I suppose. Getting over the initial learning curve of React was rough at times, I'll admit, but once I'd gotten comfortable with the idea of hooks, components, file structures, etc., I started to really get into it.

The prevailing group conflict was when a team member repeatedly claimed they had completed their work on the frontend throughout multiple scrum meetings, only to confess to the team — just days before a key deadline — that none of it was actually done. At that point, I was the only one with the React experience needed to get it done in time, so I had to take on the work myself on top of what I was already responsible for. It wasn’t ideal (some late, *late* nights), but given the situation, it was the only way we could meet the deadline. It was definitely frustrating, but we pushed through and got it done in the end. For the next sprint, our time-keepers made sure to have people check in their work frequently to ensure that development was coming along evenly - a practice we should have more strongly encouraged from the start.

We were tasked with writing up a large piece of literature to conclude the module that evaluated our system with respect to where we started. Did it solve the problem we identified, and if so, to what extent? Members of the group conducted interviews with people from the university to compare our system (being ran on the Digital Ocean Droplets) to the existing system (the Student Union website). Unsurprisingly, and much the point of the evaluations, the users sometimes discovered bugs in the system: the team member would contact me, tell me about the bug, and I'd go hunting after lectures to find the issue, test locally, push to the remote branch, wait for the tests to run, before confirming the merge into main. Our GitHub Actions script would trigger new images to be built (excluding our tests, additional dependencies, some environmental stuff) before SSH'ing into the Droplets and setting up the new system version, this time without bugs, ready for further evaluation. I'm not entirely sure how we would have done evaluations without remote deployments, and manually patching them every time we found something that needed fixing would have been pretty frustrating.

To cut the story short, data was gathered throughout very successful evaluations, and as the native Maths and Computer Science student, I crunched the numbers and did some analysis, wrote up my portion of the deliverable, and got an overall module-grade I'm very happy with.

All that said, I'm particularly proud of a few things about this project:

  • The notion that I was comfortable enough with React half-way through development to be able to take on significant additional responsibilities and a rough deadline, and still independantly produce a product that I'm very pleased with.
  • The "My First CI/CD Pipeline" I established for the group during planning and prep. I'd taken a lot of interest in how that sort of stuff had worked before, but getting to grips with Docker hands-on, utilizing remote servers properly, using multiple repositories, and getting everything to work with each other was rough at the start but definitely worth it. I'm much more keen now get a closer look at the DevOps side of industry if the opportunity arrises. The satisfaction of CI/CD working properly was something else...
  • My first serious experience in a role with some decent level of leadership in the context of CS. I was surprised at how much I really enjoyed facilitating discussion, guiding the development portion of the project, and bringing everyone's skills together.
